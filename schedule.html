Curriculum

Year 1, Q1 — starting May 1, 2026
Daily: French 20 min + Anki 15 min Daily: 20 min gesture drawing Wks 1–12: Logic (primary module) Wks 1–8: Scientific Literacy (concurrent) Wks 1–12: Math I (Saturday deep work)
Monday
Logic + Scientific Literacy
Reading block. After each section, close the book and write retrieval notes from memory. Week 1: LPL Ch. 1–2 and Sagan Ch. 1–3. No skipping the writing — that is the actual work.
Tuesday
French + Math I
French: 30 min Duolingo plus one Easy French or InnerFrench video. Math: Khan Academy exercises, one concept only. Week 1: algebra diagnostic — be honest about gaps.
Wednesday
Drawing — Phase A
Weeks 1–4: gesture sketches only. Dodson Keys to Drawing. 20–120 second poses. Use line-of-action.com for pose reference. Goal: 100 gestures by end of Phase A.
Thursday
Logic problem sets
Exercise sets from LPL. Write your reasoning in plain English before checking the answer. Build Anki fallacy deck as you go — one card per fallacy encountered.
Friday
Weekly retrieval
No notes. Write everything you remember from Logic and Sci Lit this week. Check it against the text. The gap between what you thought you knew and what you got right is exactly what you study next week.
Saturday
Math I deep work — 2.5 to 3 hours
The hard session. Weeks 1–2: algebra diagnostic. Week 3 onward: 3Blue1Brown Essence of Calculus. Attempt problems before watching solutions. Drawing long render follows.
Sunday
Sci Lit reading + weekly review + guitar
Sagan chapters, 90 min. Then 30 min weekly review: log hours, update Anki, plan next week's sessions. Guitar 30–45 min — no pressure. Just play.

What success looks like in week one

  • Logic Anki deck started with 10 cards minimum
  • LPL Ch. 1–2 read with retrieval notes written after, not during
  • Sagan Ch. 1–3 read, one Claim Check drafted on any health or science news story
  • Khan algebra diagnostic attempted and gaps identified honestly
  • 20 gesture sketches done across the week
  • French happened at least 4 days

That is a complete, successful first week. Nothing more is required.

Reframing what learning looks like

Not school

  • No deadlines except yours. If a week goes sideways, pick up where you left off
  • The deliverables are for you, not a professor
  • If a resource isn't working, the curriculum lists alternatives for every module
  • French and drawing are daily habits, not subjects — they happen the way brushing your teeth happens
  • Confusion in math means you are at your learning edge, not that something is wrong

The daily minimum that matters

  • 15 min Anki — every single day
  • 20 min French — at least 5 days a week
  • 20 min gesture drawing — at least 5 days a week
  • The scheduled study block — once a day on weekdays
  • That is 70 minutes of curriculum per day. Over a summer it becomes extraordinary

Presence practice

Daily habits for confidence, friendship, and showing up fully — not a self-improvement project, a quiet practice

The core problem and the actual fix

What you're describing — talking too fast, saying things you immediately regret, thinking about yourself in groups, not feeling genuine with people your age — is not a personality flaw. It is what self-consciousness looks like from the inside. The split attention between performing and being there is what reads as distance or awkwardness to others. The fix is not becoming someone else. It is learning to redirect attention outward rather than inward, consistently, until it becomes automatic.

Daily practices — woven into what you already do

Morning intention (30 seconds, inside morning pages or just after)
Pick one word that describes who you want to be today. Not what you want to accomplish. Who you want to be. Grounded. Curious. Present. Patient. Carry it. This is from the Stoic practice and it takes almost no time.
The one conversation practice (one conversation per day, any day)
In one conversation today, say something and do not qualify it. Make the statement. Stop. Do not add "I don't know," "if that makes sense," or "maybe I'm wrong." Let it sit. The silence after a considered statement is not awkward — it is weight. This is the single highest-leverage presence habit you can build.
The outward redirect (whenever you catch self-consciousness in a group)
The moment you notice you are watching yourself from the outside, ask: what is genuinely interesting about this person right now? Find it. Your self-consciousness disappears when your attention is fully on someone else. This is not a trick. It is a genuine practice that produces genuine connection.
The weekly reach-out (Sunday, inside weekly review)
One message to one person you have been meaning to contact. It does not need to be long or clever. "I've been bad at staying in touch and I'm trying to change that. How are you?" is enough. Most people who like you are not keeping score. They are living their own lives. A simple honest message lands far better than the months of silence that preceded it.
Evening reflection (inside your journal block, takes 2 minutes)
Three questions, briefly: What did I do well today in how I showed up to other people? What didn't reflect who I want to be? One specific thing I will do differently tomorrow. This is the Stoic evening reflection adapted to your specific goals. Do not use it to punish yourself. Use it as honest accounting.

On friendship specifically

Real friendship does not require you to be a different person. It requires you to show up as the person you are, repeatedly. People become close by accumulating shared time. The friendships you feel you have let drift are not lost unless you have decided they are. The practice is repetition and reliability: show up, remember what people tell you, follow through on what you say.

One more thing worth naming: you do not need many friends. You need a few real ones. Quality over number, always. But those few require maintenance — not constant contact, but regular genuine check-ins. A real conversation once a month is a friendship. Silence for six months followed by guilt is a pattern. The Sunday reach-out breaks the pattern.

On speaking — slowing down

The physical habits

  • Slow down by 20%. Nervous speech is fast speech. A deliberate pace reads as considered, not slow
  • Comfortable eye contact — genuine interest, not performed attentiveness
  • Ask one good question instead of several small ones — then actually listen to the full answer
  • Have positions. "Whatever you think" as a default is an absence, not a position

Stop explaining yourself

  • "I don't drink tonight" needs no explanation
  • "I prefer to head home" needs no justification
  • "I'm not available then" is a complete sentence
  • The instinct to justify is the instinct to seek approval. Practice letting your choices stand without a defence

Professional edge

Firelight — research analyst, client-facing, front of the room before you know it

The person who has it ready

The difference between "sorry, I'm still working on that" and "I have that for you right here" is almost never talent. It is the gap between doing work in your head versus doing it on paper early. Research analysts who become indispensable are not necessarily the most brilliant people in the room. They are the ones who make everyone else's job easier by being one step ahead of the ask.

The anticipation habit

  • At the end of each workday, ask: what will someone need from me tomorrow that I could prepare tonight?
  • When you finish a task, ask: what is the logical next question someone will have about this? Answer it before they ask
  • Before any client-facing moment, write down the three things they are most likely to ask. Have the answers ready
  • Keep a running doc of every recurring request — build templates so the second version takes a quarter of the time

The communication habits

  • When you send work, say what it is and what you want the recipient to do with it in the first sentence
  • Never send a problem without at least one proposed solution alongside it
  • Confirm your understanding of a task before you start it, not halfway through
  • If something will be late, say so 24 hours before it is due — not after

Being part of a team when you think about yourself in groups

What you are describing in groups — thinking too much about yourself, not feeling like part of the team — has the same root as the social self-consciousness. The fix is the same: redirect attention outward. In a work context, that means asking yourself "what does this team need right now" instead of "how am I coming across right now."

The meeting habit
Before every meeting, pick one thing you will contribute — one observation, one question, one piece of information you bring that others don't have. Say it. Then stop talking and listen fully. People who speak once with substance are remembered far more than people who fill silence.
The generous colleague habit
Once a week, do something that makes a colleague's job easier without being asked — share a resource, flag something relevant, offer to take something off someone's plate. Generosity at work builds reputation faster than any individual piece of work. It also shifts your internal orientation from "how am I doing" to "how can I help."
The end-of-day log (5 min, inside closing shift)
Three lines: what I completed today, what is outstanding and when it will be done, one thing I will do tomorrow before anyone has to ask me. This keeps you one step ahead structurally and trains the anticipation habit into an automatic system.

Client-facing confidence

Before client contact

  • Know the names of everyone you will speak to
  • Know what they care about — their priorities, not just their questions
  • Have the numbers, dates, and deliverables you are likely to be asked about accessible before the call starts
  • Take 60 seconds before going into a call to breathe and set one intention: be useful to these people

When you don't know something

  • "I don't have that in front of me right now — I'll get it to you by end of day" is professional and trustworthy
  • "I'm not sure but let me find out" followed by actually finding out is the entire job
  • Guessing to avoid looking uninformed is the only thing that actually undermines credibility
  • Saying "I'll confirm that" is a power move when you deliver
